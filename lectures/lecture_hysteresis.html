Interdisciplinary Lecture Series — No. 7

Hysteresis

When Systems Remember: Path-Dependence Across Materials Science, Ecology, and Economics
Field of Origin: Materials Science & Thermodynamics  |  Cross-Pollination: Ecological Economics  |  ~1,100 words
I. The Why

The Problem of Irreversibility

In the mid-nineteenth century, physicists studying ferromagnetic materials encountered a puzzle that classical mechanics could not resolve. When an iron specimen was magnetized by an external field and that field was subsequently removed, the iron did not return to its original unmagnetized state. It retained a "memory" of the field it had experienced. James Alfred Ewing coined the term hysteresis (from the Greek ὑστέρησις, "a lagging behind") in 1881 to name this phenomenon: the output of a system depends not merely on its current input but on the history of inputs it has received.

This was conceptually threatening. Newtonian mechanics is time-reversible; given a state and forces, you can run the clock forward or backward. Hysteresis broke that symmetry. It demanded a new language for systems in which the path taken through state-space permanently reshapes the landscape of possible futures. The concept has since migrated far beyond physics, becoming one of the most productive theoretical exports of the twentieth century.

II. Foundational Bridge

Prerequisites via Analogy

Consider a common experience: pressing a foam mattress with your hand. Push down, and it deforms. Release, and it mostly springs back—but not entirely. A faint impression remains. If you pressed harder or longer, the impression deepens. The mattress "remembers" the pressure. Now imagine that the shape of the impression changes which future pressures the mattress can absorb. That is hysteresis in miniature.

Two undergraduate-level concepts are essential here. First, state variables versus path variables: in thermodynamics, temperature is a state variable (it depends only on where you are), while heat is a path variable (it depends on how you got there). Hysteresis is fundamentally about systems where quantities you expected to be state variables turn out to be path variables. Second, nonlinearity: in a linear system, doubling the input doubles the output, and removing the input restores the original state. Hysteresis requires nonlinearity—the system's response curve on the way "up" differs from the curve on the way "down."

III. The Core Concept

Mechanism of the Hysteresis Loop

In ferromagnetic materials, the canonical domain is the B–H curve (the relationship between magnetic flux density B and the applied magnetic field intensity H). As H increases from zero, B rises nonlinearly until it reaches saturation (the point at which all magnetic domains are aligned and further increases in H produce negligible gains in B). When H is then decreased, B does not retrace its ascending path. Instead, it follows a higher curve, and when H returns to zero, a residual magnetization called remanence (Br) persists. To drive B back to zero requires a negative field called the coercivity (Hc). The complete cycle traces a closed loop—the hysteresis loop—whose enclosed area represents energy dissipated as heat during each cycle.

The physical mechanism lies in domain wall pinning. Ferromagnetic materials consist of microscopic regions (domains) of uniform magnetization. Under an applied field, domain walls shift to enlarge favorably oriented domains. But walls snag on crystalline defects, grain boundaries, and impurities. Energy must be supplied to overcome these barriers—and once overcome, the wall jumps irreversibly to a new position. This Barkhausen effect (the discontinuous, stochastic jumps in magnetization) means the system cannot simply retrace its steps when the field reverses. The microstructural disorder of the material encodes a history.

Formally, hysteresis arises whenever a system possesses multiple metastable states (local energy minima separated by barriers) and the transitions between them are thermodynamically irreversible at the timescale of observation. This abstract formulation is what makes the concept portable across disciplines.

IV. Cross-Pollination

Hysteresis in Ecological and Economic Systems

In ecology, the concept of hysteresis transformed how we understand regime shifts (abrupt, large-scale transitions between alternative stable states of an ecosystem). The work of C.S. Holling (1973) and later Marten Scheffer (2001) demonstrated that shallow lakes, coral reefs, and arid grasslands exhibit hysteretic dynamics. A clear lake, for example, can be pushed by nutrient loading (phosphorus runoff) into a turbid, algae-dominated state. But reducing phosphorus back to the level at which the transition occurred does not restore clarity. The system has reorganized: sediment now releases stored phosphorus, fish populations have shifted to favor turbidity, and submerged vegetation has died. Recovery requires pushing phosphorus concentrations far below the original tipping point—a phenomenon ecologists call the recovery debt.

The structural parallel to the B–H curve is precise. Nutrient loading maps onto H; water clarity maps onto B. The ascending and descending paths do not coincide. The "coercivity" is the additional remediation effort required beyond merely reversing the original stressor. The "remanence" is the degraded state that persists even after the stressor is nominally removed. And the enclosed area of the loop represents the cumulative cost—economic, ecological, temporal—of the round trip.

Economics absorbed the concept through the work of Blanchard and Summers (1986) on unemployment hysteresis: prolonged recessions do not merely suppress employment temporarily but permanently raise the "natural" rate of unemployment. Workers lose skills, firms dissolve supplier networks, and labor-market institutions adapt to the new equilibrium. The policy implication is severe—austerity applied too aggressively creates structural damage that subsequent stimulus cannot cheaply reverse, precisely because the descending path lies above the ascending one. The loop area, here, is measured in years of lost human capital and output.

What unifies these applications is the formal structure: a system with internal degrees of freedom that reorganize under stress, creating barriers to reversal. Whether those internal degrees of freedom are magnetic domains, phosphorus-cycling microbes, or long-term unemployed workers, the mathematics of path-dependent switching between metastable attractors is remarkably conserved. Hysteresis thus serves as one of the most powerful examples of how a concept born in the physics of iron can illuminate why ecosystems collapse, why recessions scar, and why the naive assumption that "removing the cause removes the effect" is, in complex systems, almost always wrong.
