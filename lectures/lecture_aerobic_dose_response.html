Aerobic Exercise — Three Dose-Response Curves, One Weekly Architecture

Published June 2026  ·  Science in this area continues to evolve — this lecture reflects research available at the time of writing. Consult a qualified clinician before making health decisions.

The Same 30-Minute Run Produces Three Different Dose-Response Curves Depending on Which Endpoint You Measure — All-Cause Mortality (Logarithmic, No Confirmed Reversal), VO₂ Max Improvement (Sigmoidal, Threshold + Plateau), and Hippocampal Volume (Low Threshold, Early Saturation). I. The Core Concept — Three Curves From One Intervention

Each Endpoint Has Its Own EC₅₀, Its Own Emax, and Its Own Therapeutic Window

Curve 1: All-cause mortality — logarithmic, wide therapeutic window, no confirmed ceiling. The definitive dataset: Arem et al. (2015, JAMA Internal Medicine, pooled analysis, n=661,137) mapped the exercise-mortality dose-response with extraordinary precision. Compared to no leisure-time physical activity: 1–2× the recommended minimum (150–299 min/week moderate) produced a 31% mortality reduction; 2–3× the minimum (300–449 min/week) produced a 37% reduction; 3–5× the minimum (450–749 min/week) produced a 39% reduction; and even 10× the minimum (>750 min/week) showed no excess mortality. The curve's shape is logarithmic — a steep initial drop that flattens progressively without reverting. The key inflection: 75 minutes per week of vigorous activity — approximately 15 minutes per day, 5 days per week — is the MED that crosses the first meaningful mortality reduction threshold. Performing at least 75 min per week of physical activity contributed to significantly reduced all-cause mortality. Below this threshold, individuals are in the high-risk zone where the steepest per-minute benefit exists. The Optimal Dose — the efficiency frontier where the curve transitions from steep to flat — is approximately 150–300 minutes per week of moderate activity (or 75–150 minutes of vigorous activity). Beyond 300 minutes, returns diminish substantially but remain positive. The therapeutic window is enormous: the MED is 75 min/week and no MTD has been confirmed for the general population — making aerobic exercise the pharmacological equivalent of a drug with a 10:1+ therapeutic index.

Curve 2: VO₂ max improvement — sigmoidal, threshold-dependent, intensity-gated. VO₂ max — the single strongest predictor of cardiovascular longevity (Lecture 16) — follows a fundamentally different dose-response shape from mortality. The curve is sigmoidal with two critical thresholds: an intensity threshold (below ~60% of max HR, minimal VO₂ max adaptation occurs regardless of volume — this is the lag phase where walking alone produces minimal cardiovascular fitness improvement) and a volume ceiling (above which additional training produces negligible further VO₂ max gain — typically reached at 5–6 hours/week of structured training in a person in their fifties). Between these boundaries, two training modalities drive VO₂ max through different mechanisms. Zone 2 training (60–70% of max HR — the highest intensity at which lactate production equals lactate clearance, maximizing mitochondrial density, capillary density, and fat oxidation capacity in slow-twitch muscle fibers) builds the aerobic base: mitochondrial volume, fatty acid transport, capillary network density. It responds to high volume (3–5 sessions/week, 30–60 min each). HIIT (high-intensity interval training — brief intervals at 85–95% of max HR interspersed with recovery periods, which drives stroke volume increase and cardiac output expansion through different hemodynamic adaptations than Zone 2) drives the peak: maximal stroke volume, left ventricular compliance, peak cardiac output. Every 1 MET increase in CRF is associated with an 11% reduction in the relative risk for all-cause mortality — making VO₂ max improvement per MET one of the most powerful dose-response relationships in medicine. HIIT responds to low frequency but high intensity (1–2 sessions/week, 20–30 min). The weekly architecture that optimizes the VO₂ max sigmoid: 3–4 sessions of Zone 2 (building the base) + 1–2 sessions of HIIT (building the peak).

Curve 3: Hippocampal volume and BDNF — low threshold, early saturation. The Erickson et al. (2011) trial demonstrated that 120 minutes per week of moderate walking — 40 minutes, 3×/week — produced a 2% increase in hippocampal volume in older adults over one year, while the control group showed a 1.4% decrease. This is a lower dose than either the mortality-optimal or VO₂ max-optimal prescription — suggesting that the cognitive dose-response curve has a lower EC₅₀ than the cardiovascular curve. The BDNF dose-response appears to have a threshold at approximately 20 minutes of moderate activity (below which minimal BDNF elevation occurs) and a plateau above approximately 45–60 minutes per session (beyond which additional duration does not further increase circulating BDNF). The implication: the cognitive protection documented in Lectures 31 and 52 requires a lower exercise dose than cardiovascular optimization — a person who can only manage three 40-minute walks per week is still capturing most of the cognitive benefit, even if they're below the VO₂ max optimization threshold. This is the most encouraging dose-response finding for individuals starting from a low baseline: the brain responds to less exercise than the heart requires.

II. Cross-Pollination — Multi-Endpoint Optimization: The Combination Drug Analogy
Exercise Physiology

The VPA Proportion Curve — A Fourth Optimization

A 2024 meta-analysis revealed that maintaining vigorous physical activity at 30–60% of total MVPA is associated with maximal reduction in all-cause and CVD mortality. Below 30% VPA, the dose is too mild to drive VO₂ max adaptations. Above 60% VPA, the proportion of intense training may exceed recovery capacity and increase cortisol/immune costs. The ratio itself — not just total volume — follows a U-shaped optimum. The weekly architecture (3–4× Zone 2 + 1–2× HIIT) naturally produces a VPA proportion of ~25–35%, near the lower edge of the optimal window. Adding a third HIIT session would push VPA toward 40–50% — still within the optimal window but increasing recovery demands.

Clinical Pharmacology

The Combination Drug: Why Z2 + HIIT Outperforms Either Alone

In pharmacology, combination therapy uses drugs with different mechanisms to produce complementary effects (e.g., ACE inhibitor + diuretic for hypertension). Zone 2 and HIIT are pharmacologically analogous to two drugs with different targets: Z2 drives peripheral adaptation (mitochondrial density in type I fibers, capillary angiogenesis, fat oxidation enzymes — the oxygen-extraction machinery). HIIT drives central adaptation (stroke volume increase, cardiac output expansion — the oxygen-delivery machinery). VO₂ max = delivery × extraction. Maximizing both requires both stimuli — at their respective optimal doses, not at the max of either.

Exercise Physiology

The 1-MET Mortality Currency

A meta-analysis of 37 cohort studies (>2 million adults) revealed that every 1 MET increase in cardiorespiratory fitness is associated with an 11% reduction in relative risk for all-cause mortality — independent of age, sex, and follow-up duration. This makes the MET the "currency" of cardiorespiratory longevity: each MET gained through training purchases an 11% mortality reduction. For a person in their fifties, increasing VO₂ max from 8 METs to 10 METs (a realistic 12-month training goal) translates to a ~22% mortality risk reduction — comparable to the effect of a statin on cardiovascular events.

Clinical Pharmacology

Adherence Modifies Effective Dose

In drug trials, adherence rates typically run 50–80% — meaning the effective dose received is lower than the prescribed dose. The same applies to exercise protocols: a 5-day/week prescription followed 60% of the time produces a 3-day/week effective dose. The Protocol Efficiency Ratio from Lecture 61 must account for this: a 3-day/week prescription followed 95% of the time may produce greater benefit than a 5-day/week prescription followed 60% of the time. The optimal prescribed dose is the dose that maximizes effective dose × adherence probability — not the dose that maximizes benefit at perfect compliance.

III. Antifragility & Trade-Offs
Where the Curves Conflict — and How to Resolve the Tension

The three curves produce different optimal doses — and the conflicts must be explicitly resolved. The mortality curve says 150–300 min/week is the efficiency frontier. The VO₂ max curve says structured intensity matters more than volume — 1–2 HIIT sessions + 3–4 Zone 2 sessions, totaling ~250–350 min/week. The brain curve says 120 min/week is sufficient. A person with unlimited time could satisfy all three by exercising ~350 min/week (5 sessions: 3 Zone 2 × 45 min + 2 HIIT × 25 min). But a person with 150 min/week available faces a genuine optimization problem: should they spread 150 min across 5 low-intensity sessions (maximizing the mortality curve), concentrate it into 3 moderate-intensity sessions (satisfying the brain curve), or split it between 2 Zone 2 + 1 HIIT (beginning to address the VO₂ max curve)? The answer depends on endpoint priority — but the general principle from Lecture 61 applies: operate at the EC₅₀ of multiple curves rather than the Emax of one. For 150 min/week: 2× Zone 2 (40 min) + 1× HIIT (20 min) + 2× brisk walk (25 min) captures ~70% of the mortality benefit, ~50% of the VO₂ max potential, and ~90% of the brain benefit — a PER >1 across all three endpoints. The concurrent training consideration from Lecture 63 (resistance training interaction with aerobic) adds a fourth constraint that must be resolved by weekly sequencing.

IV. The Protocol — Specific Numbers for a Person in Their Fifties
The Weekly Architecture With Dose Parameters

The Prescription: Three tiers based on available time budget:

Tier 1 — Time-Rich (350+ min/wk available): Zone 2: 4×/wk × 45 min (180 min). HIIT: 2×/wk × 25 min (50 min). Total: ~300 min moderate equivalent. This operates in the optimal zone of all three curves simultaneously. VO₂ max improvement: target 1–2 METs/year (11–22% mortality reduction per MET). Combine with resistance training (Lecture 63) on 2–3 of the Zone 2 days (separate sessions by >3 hours or do resistance AM, Zone 2 PM).

Tier 2 — Time-Moderate (200 min/wk available): Zone 2: 3×/wk × 40 min (120 min). HIIT: 1×/wk × 25 min (25 min). Brisk walk: 2×/wk × 25 min (50 min). Total: ~220 min moderate equivalent. This captures ~85% of the mortality benefit, ~70% of VO₂ max potential, and >90% of brain benefit.

Tier 3 — Time-Constrained (120 min/wk available): Zone 2: 2×/wk × 30 min (60 min). HIIT: 1×/wk × 20 min (20 min). Daily walk: 7×/wk × 10 min (70 min). Total: ~170 min moderate equivalent. This is above the MED for all three curves and captures ~75% of mortality benefit, ~50% of VO₂ max, and ~80% of brain benefit. The daily 10-minute walk is the highest-PER single activity — minimal time investment, maximum per-minute mortality benefit on the steepest part of the logarithmic curve.

Zone Definitions: Zone 2 = 60–70% max HR (estimate: 220 − age × 0.60 to 0.70; for age 55: ~99–115 bpm). Conversational pace — you can speak in sentences but not sing. HIIT = 85–95% max HR intervals (for age 55: ~140–157 bpm) × 30–60 seconds, with 1–2 min recovery between intervals, 4–8 intervals per session. Vigorous = 70–85% max HR (~115–140 bpm for age 55) — this is the zone where most "running" and "cycling at effort" occurs.

Metric: VO₂ max (direct measurement via cardiopulmonary exercise test, or estimated from a timed 1-mile walk, Cooper 12-minute run, or wearable algorithm). Target for a 55-year-old male: ≥10 METs (35 mL/kg/min) places you in the top quartile for age; ≥12 METs (42 mL/kg/min) approaches the "fitness age" of a 40-year-old. Each 1 MET gained = 11% mortality risk reduction. Track quarterly. Complement with resting heart rate trend (declining = improving stroke volume), HRV/RMSSD (Lecture 47), and the Protocol Efficiency Ratio from Lecture 61.

VI. Peer Review — Common Misconception
Corrected Misconception

Misconception: "You need to exercise for at least 30 minutes for it to 'count' — shorter sessions are worthless."

Correction: The dose-response curve for all-cause mortality is logarithmic with its steepest region at the lowest doses. The first 10–15 minutes of daily exercise produce the largest per-minute mortality reduction of any segment of the curve. A 2022 study in the European Heart Journal (Stamatakis et al.) showed that even 3–4 bouts of vigorous intermittent lifestyle physical activity (VILPA) — essentially vigorous activities lasting 1–2 minutes embedded in daily life (taking stairs quickly, brief walking sprints, carrying heavy bags) — totaling as little as 3.4 minutes per day were associated with a 38–40% reduction in all-cause mortality and up to a 49% reduction in cardiovascular mortality compared to no vigorous activity. The "30-minute threshold" is a public health guideline designed for simplicity, not a physiological requirement. The biology follows the curve, not the guideline — and the curve says that every minute of movement above zero produces a disproportionately large return when starting from inactivity. The person who says "I don't have 30 minutes" can be told: "You have 10. That covers 50–70% of the mortality benefit that 30 minutes would provide, because the curve is steepest at the bottom."

Activity
Implement the Weekly Architecture

Choose your tier (1/2/3) based on available time. Schedule Zone 2, HIIT, and walking sessions into your weekly calendar with specific days and times. The architecture must be scheduled, not aspirational — implementation intention ("I will do Zone 2 on Mon/Wed/Fri at 7 AM") increases adherence by 91% compared to unscheduled intention (Gollwitzer, 1999).

Activity
Determine Your Zone Boundaries

Max HR estimate: 220 − age (crude) or 208 − (0.7 × age) (Tanaka formula, more accurate). Zone 2: 60–70% of max HR. HIIT: 85–95% of max HR. Use a chest strap HR monitor or wearable for real-time zone confirmation. For age 55: Zone 2 ≈ 99–115 bpm; HIIT ≈ 140–157 bpm (Tanaka: max HR ≈ 169.5).

Tool
The 1-MET Currency Converter

Each 1 MET increase in VO₂ max = 11% reduction in all-cause mortality risk. Current VO₂ max estimate (from wearable or Cooper test) → target 1–2 MET improvement over 12 months → translate to HR: 22% mortality reduction for 2 METs gained. This conversion makes abstract "fitness improvement" concrete and motivating.

Tool
The Floor Protocol

When life intervenes (travel, illness, stress): the absolute minimum that maintains gains = 10 min daily walking + 1× HIIT per week. This keeps you above the MED for all three curves, prevents detraining, and can be performed anywhere without equipment. The floor protocol preserves ~60% of the full protocol's benefit at ~25% of the time investment — a PER of ~2.4.

Metric
VO₂ Max + RHR + HRV + Zone Minutes

VO₂ max (quarterly — target ≥10 METs for age 55; each MET gained = 11% mortality↓). Resting HR (weekly — declining trend confirms ↑stroke volume). HRV/RMSSD (nightly — confirms autonomic adaptation). Weekly zone minutes from wearable (confirms actual dose matches prescribed dose — the adherence check). Track VPA proportion: aim 30–60% of total MVPA.
