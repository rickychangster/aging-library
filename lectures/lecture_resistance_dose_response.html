Resistance Training — Volume, Frequency, Intensity & the Sarcopenia Threshold

Published June 2026  ·  Science in this area continues to evolve — this lecture reflects research available at the time of writing. Consult a qualified clinician before making health decisions.

Aerobic Exercise (Lecture 62) Operates on Logarithmic and Sigmoidal Curves With Enormous Therapeutic Windows. Resistance Training Operates on a Different Geometry: the Volume-Hypertrophy Curve Is Approximately Linear Up to ~12 Sets Per Muscle Group Per Week Then Shows Diminishing Returns; the Frequency Curve Is Flat After 2×/Week When Volume Is Matched; and the Intensity Curve Has a Hard Threshold Below Which No Meaningful Strength Adaptation Occurs. These Three Curves, Combined With the Concurrent Training Interference Window From the mTOR-AMPK Axis (Lecture 58), Produce a Specific Weekly Architecture That Complements — Rather Than Competes With — the Aerobic Architecture of Lecture 62

I. The Core Concept — Three Independent Dose Variables, Each With Its Own Curve

Volume, Frequency, and Intensity Are Not Interchangeable — Each Has a Distinct Dose-Response Shape

Variable 1: Volume (sets per muscle group per week) — approximately linear with diminishing returns. The Pelland et al. 2025 meta-regression (Sports Medicine) — the most comprehensive dose-response analysis of resistance training volume to date — established that each additional weekly set per muscle group (using the fractional quantification method) produced approximately 0.24% additional hypertrophy (95% CrI: 0.15–0.33%) at the average volume of ~12 sets/week. This marginal return is approximately linear through the measured range — but the absolute magnitude of each additional set's contribution diminishes relative to total accumulated hypertrophy. The Schoenfeld et al. (2017) meta-analysis reported a comparable 0.38% increase per additional set using direct measurements. The practical implication: 10–12 sets per muscle group per week captures the steep-to-flat transition zone — the efficiency frontier where each set still produces meaningful adaptation without excessive volume that extends session duration, increases injury risk, and exceeds recovery capacity. Below 5 sets/week: measurable but sub-optimal hypertrophy. Between 5–12: the efficiency zone. Between 12–20: continued gains but at diminishing rates. Above 20: the evidence for additional benefit is weak, and some data suggest possible inverted-U attenuation — though this remains debated.

Variable 2: Frequency (sessions per muscle group per week) — flat above 2× when volume is matched. This is the most counterintuitive finding in resistance training dose-response: training frequency appears to be secondary to total weekly volume for hypertrophy. A person performing 10 sets for chest across 2 sessions (5 sets each) and a person performing 10 sets across 3 sessions (~3.3 sets each) produce equivalent hypertrophy — because the total stimulus (weekly volume) is identical. Thrice-weekly resistance exercise does not provide an additive benefit on sarcopenia outcomes over twice-weekly when volume is matched. The practical implication: for a person in their fifties managing concurrent aerobic training, time constraints, and recovery demands, 2–3 sessions per week is sufficient when total weekly volume per muscle group reaches 10–12 sets. Higher frequency (4×/week) allows lower per-session volume (reducing fatigue and injury risk per session) but does not produce greater hypertrophy if total weekly volume is equal. The MED for frequency is 2×/week — below which the per-session volume required to reach 10+ sets becomes impractical and potentially injurious.

Variable 3: Intensity (% of 1RM) — threshold with bifurcated endpoints. Intensity follows a threshold curve, but the threshold differs for strength vs. hypertrophy. For strength gain (neural adaptation — motor unit recruitment, rate coding, intermuscular coordination): loads ≥60% 1RM are needed for meaningful adaptation, and the dose-response continues to favor heavier loads (~70–85% 1RM for maximal strength development) because strength is fundamentally a neural phenomenon that requires high-threshold motor unit recruitment. For hypertrophy (muscle fiber cross-sectional area increase): the threshold is lower — loads as low as 30% 1RM produce equivalent hypertrophy to 80% 1RM when taken to or near muscular failure (Schoenfeld et al., 2017, J Strength Cond Res). The equalizer is proximity to failure: light loads must be performed for more reps to reach the same degree of motor unit fatigue that heavier loads achieve in fewer reps. For a person in their fifties, the optimal intensity range is 60–80% 1RM, 6–15 repetitions per set — heavy enough to recruit high-threshold motor units (driving strength and neural adaptation) but not so heavy that joint stress, injury risk, and recovery time become limiting. The Bayesian network meta-analysis for sarcopenia-specific grip strength improvement (2025) identified an optimal intensity of ~49% 1RM — lower than the hypertrophy optimum, suggesting that for the sarcopenia prevention endpoint specifically, moderate loads with higher reps may be more effective than heavy loads with low reps.

II. Cross-Pollination — Manufacturing: Throughput, Batch Frequency & Quality Assurance
Exercise Science

Anabolic Resistance — the Aging-Specific Modifier

Older muscle exhibits anabolic resistance — a blunted MPS response to the same training stimulus that would maximally stimulate a younger muscle. A 25-year-old achieves maximal MPS with ~20g protein post-exercise; a 55-year-old requires ~30–40g (Lecture 65 will detail the protein dose-response). This means the same 10-set training session produces less hypertrophy per set in an older adult — the "yield per batch" is lower. The compensation: slightly higher volume (12 vs. 10 sets), higher protein dose (40g vs. 20g post-exercise), and adequate leucine (≥2.5g per meal — the mTOR activation threshold for anabolic-resistant muscle).

Manufacturing Engineering

Batch Size vs. Batch Frequency

In manufacturing, the same weekly output can be achieved through fewer large batches or more frequent small batches. The quality difference depends on the process: some processes produce better yield from large, uninterrupted runs; others benefit from smaller, more frequent runs with quality checks between batches. Resistance training is the latter: 10 sets distributed across 3 sessions (3-3-4) allows recovery and quality control between batches, producing equivalent or better adaptation than 10 sets in one marathon session — with lower per-session fatigue, lower injury risk, and better movement quality in later sets.

Exercise Science

Proximity to Failure — the True Intensity Variable

Proximity to failure (measured as RIR — reps in reserve) may be more important than absolute load. A set of 8 reps at 80% 1RM with 0 RIR (taken to failure) produces equivalent hypertrophy to a set of 25 reps at 30% 1RM with 0 RIR — because both recruit the full motor unit pool at the point of failure. The practical implication for a person in their fifties: training at 60–75% 1RM with 1–3 RIR (stopping 1–3 reps before failure) provides sufficient stimulus for hypertrophy and strength while preserving joints, reducing injury risk, and allowing adequate recovery. Training to absolute failure on every set is unnecessary and counterproductive for long-term protocol sustainability.

Manufacturing Engineering

The Deload as Scheduled Maintenance

Manufacturing lines schedule preventive maintenance shutdowns — brief periods where production stops to inspect, repair, and recalibrate equipment, preventing the unplanned breakdowns that would halt production for much longer. The resistance training deload week (every 3–4 weeks: reduce volume by 40–50% and/or intensity by 10–15%, maintain frequency) serves the same function: allowing tendons, ligaments, and neural systems to recover from cumulative fatigue, preventing the overuse injuries that would require weeks-to-months of unplanned cessation. For a person over 50 — whose recovery biology is slower (satellite cell latency, reduced GH, reduced testosterone) — the deload is not optional. It is the scheduled maintenance that keeps the structural system operational.

III. Antifragility & Trade-Offs
Where the Resistance and Aerobic Prescriptions Conflict — and How to Resolve Them

The mTOR-AMPK interference is real but manageable with temporal separation. Four practical resolution strategies for a person in their fifties doing both aerobic (Lecture 62) and resistance training: (1) Alternate days — the simplest solution. Mon/Wed/Fri: resistance. Tue/Thu: Zone 2 aerobic. Sat: HIIT or long Zone 2. Sun: rest or active recovery. No interference because sessions are separated by 24+ hours. (2) Same-day split — resistance in the morning (when testosterone and cortisol ratios favor anabolism), aerobic in the evening (separated by ≥6 hours). AMPK activation from the evening aerobic session has largely cleared by the next morning's resistance session. (3) Post-resistance aerobic — if sessions must be combined, do resistance first (when glycogen is full and neural output is highest), followed by aerobic. This order preserves the mTOR activation window from resistance before AMPK from aerobic engages — though some interference still occurs. Never do aerobic first then resistance — the AMPK activation from aerobic directly suppresses the mTOR response to the subsequent resistance session. (4) Intensity matching — low-intensity aerobic (Zone 2 walking, light cycling) produces less AMPK activation than HIIT, creating less interference. Pairing resistance training with Zone 2 aerobic on the same day produces less mTOR suppression than pairing with HIIT. Save HIIT for separate days from heavy resistance sessions.

IV. The Protocol — Specific Numbers Integrated With Lecture 62's Aerobic Architecture
The Combined Aerobic + Resistance Weekly Architecture

The Prescription — Two Templates:

Template A — Alternate Days (preferred for maximal adaptation, minimal interference):
Mon: Resistance — upper body (chest, back, shoulders, arms) — 4 movements × 3 sets = 12 sets. 60–80% 1RM, 6–12 reps, 1–3 RIR.
Tue: Zone 2 aerobic — 40–50 min, 60–70% max HR.
Wed: Resistance — lower body (squat, deadlift, lunge, calf raise) — 4 movements × 3 sets = 12 sets. 60–80% 1RM, 6–15 reps, 1–3 RIR.
Thu: Zone 2 aerobic — 40–50 min.
Fri: Resistance — full body (compound movements, moderate volume) — 5 movements × 2 sets = 10 sets. 60–75% 1RM, 8–15 reps.
Sat: HIIT — 25 min (4–8 intervals at 85–95% max HR) OR long Zone 2 (60–90 min).
Sun: Rest or active recovery (walk, mobility, sauna).

Volume check: upper body = 12 direct sets + 10 from full-body compounds ≈ 10–12 sets/muscle group/wk. Lower body = 12 direct + 10 full-body ≈ 10–12 sets/muscle group/wk. ✓ Optimal zone.
Aerobic check: Z2 ~100 min + HIIT ~25 min + walks = ~200–250 min moderate equivalent/wk. ✓ Lecture 62 optimal zone.

Template B — Time-Constrained (3 sessions/wk, concurrent training):
Mon/Wed/Fri: Resistance (35–40 min) → Zone 2 aerobic (20–30 min). Resistance first, always. Total session: 55–70 min.
Resistance: 3 full-body sessions × 5 movements × 2–3 sets = 10–15 sets/muscle group/wk across the week.
Aerobic: 3 × 25 min Z2 = 75 min + add 1 standalone HIIT session (Sat, 20 min) = ~120 min moderate equivalent/wk. ✓ Above MED for all Lecture 62 curves.

Deload schedule: Every 4th week: reduce sets by 40% (e.g., 12 sets → 7 sets/muscle group), reduce intensity by 10% (e.g., 75% → 65% 1RM), maintain frequency and movement patterns. Resume full volume in week 5. This 3:1 loading:deload ratio prevents cumulative fatigue, preserves tendon/ligament health, and allows the supercompensation (Lecture 67) that produces continued adaptation.

Metric: Grip strength (Lecture 59 — $25 dynamometer, quarterly tracking, best of 3 attempts dominant hand). Progressive overload log — the most important resistance training metric is whether the total load lifted (sets × reps × weight) is trending upward over months. A plateau lasting >4 weeks despite adequate sleep, nutrition, and recovery signals the need for programming adjustment (periodization change, exercise variation, volume modification). Body composition — DEXA (annually) or waist-to-hip ratio (monthly) to confirm that lean mass is maintained or increasing while visceral fat is stable or declining. 1RM estimates — for the five compound movements, estimate 1RM from submaximal performance (Epley or Brzycki formula) quarterly. Declining 1RM trend = strength loss = safety margin erosion (Lecture 59).

VI. Peer Review — Common Misconception
Corrected Misconception

Misconception: "Older adults should use light weights with high reps to avoid injury — heavy lifting is dangerous after 50."

Correction: The evidence shows the opposite: moderate-to-heavy resistance training (60–80% 1RM) produces superior strength gains compared to light loads in older adults, and the injury rate in supervised resistance training programs for older adults is extremely low (0.2–3.3 injuries per 1,000 participant-hours — comparable to walking). The misconception confuses training to failure with heavy loads (which does increase injury risk, particularly for joints and connective tissue) with training at moderate-to-heavy loads with controlled reps in reserve (which is both safe and more effective than light loads for the strength and neural adaptations that prevent falls and maintain functional independence). The 2025 Bayesian meta-analysis for sarcopenia specifically identified ~49% 1RM as optimal for grip strength improvement — a moderate load, not a light one. Furthermore, heavy compound movements (squat, deadlift) produce the highest osteoblast-stimulating mechanical loads documented in Lecture 43 — loads that light weights with high reps cannot replicate. The prescription for a person in their fifties is not "lift light to be safe" — it is "lift heavy enough to stimulate adaptation (≥60% 1RM), with enough reps in reserve to be sustainable (1–3 RIR), with scheduled deloads to prevent cumulative fatigue (every 4th week)." This is the evidence-based resolution of the safety-effectiveness tension.

Activity
Implement Template A or B

Choose based on available time. Template A (5–6 days, alternate): maximal adaptation, zero interference. Template B (3 days, concurrent): time-efficient, moderate interference managed by session order (resistance first). Schedule specific days, times, and movements. Track every session in a training log.

Activity
Establish 1RM Baselines

For the five compound movements (squat, deadlift, row, overhead press, lunge): find your estimated 1RM using submaximal testing (a set of 5–8 reps to near-failure; apply Epley formula: 1RM = weight × (1 + reps/30)). This establishes your intensity zones: 60% 1RM, 70%, 80%. Retest quarterly to track strength trajectory.

Tool
The Deload Protocol

Every 4th week: same exercises, same frequency, but reduce volume by 40% (3 sets → 2 sets per movement) and intensity by ~10% (75% → 65% 1RM). Maintain movement patterns to preserve neural coordination. This is not a rest week — it is a recovery week that allows tendons, joints, and the nervous system to supercompensate before the next loading block.

Tool
The Five-Movement Foundation

Squat pattern (barbell back squat, goblet squat, leg press). Hip hinge (conventional or trap bar deadlift, Romanian deadlift). Horizontal push (bench press, push-up). Horizontal/vertical pull (barbell row, pull-up, lat pulldown). Lunge/single-leg (walking lunge, Bulgarian split squat, step-up). These five patterns load every major muscle group through functional movement planes. Master form before adding load.

Metric
Grip + 1RM Trend + Lean Mass + Overload Log

Grip strength (dynamometer, quarterly). Estimated 1RM for 5 compounds (quarterly — declining = safety margin erosion). DEXA lean mass (annually) or waist-hip ratio (monthly). Training log (progressive overload confirmation — total volume should trend upward over 3-month blocks). Deload compliance (every 4th week — skipping deloads is the most common programming error in middle-aged trainees).
